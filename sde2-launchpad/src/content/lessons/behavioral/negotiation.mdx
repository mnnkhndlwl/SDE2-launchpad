The offer is the start of a conversation, not the end. Companies expect negotiation and budget for it; not negotiating usually just leaves money on the table.

## Before any number is said

- **Know your worth.** Research the band for the role, level, and location (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, peers). Know total comp: base + bonus + equity + benefits, not just base.
- **Know your number.** Decide your target and your walk-away *before* you're on the call, so you're not improvising emotionally.
- **Don't anchor yourself low.** When asked "what are you expecting?" early, deflect: "I'd like to learn more about the role and scope first — I'm sure we can find a number that works." If pressed, give a researched range with your target near the bottom.

## Leverage

- **Competing offers** are the strongest lever — line up your interviews to cluster, so offers overlap. Even one other process in progress helps.
- **Let them make the first offer** when you can. The first number anchors the range.
- **Sell your value** continuously through the loop; negotiation is easier when they're excited about you.

## When the offer comes

- **Don't accept on the spot**, even if thrilled. "Thank you, I'm really excited. Can I have a few days to review?" Buys time and signals you're thoughtful.
- **Counter with a specific, justified number**, on total comp, politely and confidently: "Based on my research for this level and my competing conversations, I was hoping for X. Is there room to get there?"
- **Negotiate the whole package** — if base is capped, push on sign-on bonus, equity, start date, or level. Level matters most: a higher level compounds for years.
- **Get it in writing** before you resign anywhere.

## Mistakes to avoid

- Giving a hard number first, too low.
- Accepting immediately out of relief or fear.
- Negotiating without any leverage or data.
- Being adversarial — stay warm and collaborative; you'll work with these people.
- Bluffing a competing offer you don't have (it can backfire badly).

  <div class="ct">The mindset</div>
  Negotiating respectfully does not put the offer at risk — rescinding over a polite counter is extremely rare. Be warm, specific, and data-backed, and remember the level you land at compounds for your whole tenure.
