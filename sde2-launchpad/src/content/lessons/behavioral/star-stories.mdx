Behavioral questions probe the same handful of themes. You don't prepare an answer per question — you prepare **6–8 strong stories** and map them onto whatever's asked.

## The STAR structure

Each story is four beats, kept tight:

- **Situation** — one or two sentences of context. Don't over-explain.
- **Task** — what *you* specifically were responsible for.
- **Action** — what *you* did (use "I", not "we"). This is the bulk; show your reasoning and the tradeoffs you weighed.
- **Result** — the outcome, **quantified** where possible ("cut p95 latency 40%", "shipped 2 weeks early", "reduced crash rate from 3% to 0.4%"). End with what you learned.

## The stories to prepare

Cover these themes; most questions map to one:

| Theme | Prompt it answers |
|---|---|
| A failure / mistake | "Tell me about a time you failed" |
| A conflict / disagreement | "...you disagreed with a teammate/manager" |
| Ownership / initiative | "...you went beyond your role" |
| A hard technical problem | "...the hardest bug / problem you solved" |
| Impact / a win | "...your most significant contribution" |
| Ambiguity / learning fast | "...you had to learn something quickly" |
| Leadership / influence | "...you influenced without authority" |

Two or three of your stories can cover multiple themes — that's fine, and efficient.

## What separates strong from weak

- **Specificity.** Real names of systems, real numbers, real decisions. Vague stories read as fabricated.
- **"I", not "we".** Interviewers need *your* contribution. Credit the team, but be clear what you did.
- **Show the tradeoff.** "I chose X over Y because…" demonstrates judgment, the thing they're actually grading.
- **Own failures honestly.** A real failure with a real lesson beats a humble-brag ("I work too hard"). Show what you changed afterward.

<div class="callout warn">
  <div class="ct">The conflict question trap</div>
  Don't pick a story where you "won" by steamrolling. Pick one where you listened, found data or a shared goal, and reached a good outcome together. They're testing collaboration, not stubbornness.
</div>
